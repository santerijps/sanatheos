Mariology Cheat Sheet

A concise reference to the Orthodox Christian teaching on the Most Holy Theotokos and Ever-Virgin Mary.

Theotokos — Mother of God

The title Theotokos (Θεοτόκος, “God-bearer” or “Mother of God”) is not primarily a Marian title but a Christological one. It safeguards the truth that the One born of Mary is not a mere man to whom divinity was later added, but the eternal God-Logos incarnate. Mary bore God in the flesh—one Person who is both divine and human.

To deny the title Theotokos is to divide Christ into two persons (the Nestorian error). To affirm it is to confess the hypostatic union: the Person born of Mary is the same Person who was begotten of the Father before all ages.

Councils & Fathers

  • Third Ecumenical Council (Ephesus, 431) — solemnly affirmed the title Theotokos and condemned Nestorius for substituting Christotokos (“Christ-bearer”).
  • St. Cyril of Alexandria — the principal champion of the title at Ephesus; his Twelve Anathemas defend the inseparable unity of Christ’s Person.
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — “If anyone does not accept the holy Mary as Theotokos, he is without the Godhead” (Epistle 101).
  • Sub Tuum Praesidium (3rd century) — the oldest known Marian hymn, addressing Mary as Theotokos: “Beneath thy compassion we take refuge, O Theotokos.”

Ever-Virginity (Aeiparthenos)

The Orthodox Church confesses that Mary was a virgin before, during, and after the birth of Christ (ante partum, in partu, post partum). This is the dogma of the Ever-Virginity (Aeiparthenos) of the Theotokos. The “brothers” of Jesus mentioned in Scripture are understood as either children of Joseph from a previous marriage or close relatives (cousins), following ancient tradition.

Mary’s virginity is not merely a biological fact but a theological sign: it manifests the wholly divine initiative in the Incarnation. The conception of Christ is entirely the work of the Holy Spirit; the new creation enters the world not by the will of man but by the will of God (John 1:13).

Fathers

  • St. Athanasius of Alexandria — affirmed Mary’s perpetual virginity as integral to the mystery of the Incarnation.
  • St. Jerome — defended the perpetual virginity against Helvidius in Against Helvidius (383).
  • The Protoevangelium of James (2nd century) — an early text reflecting the Church’s belief in Mary’s virginity and the tradition that Joseph was an elderly widower with children.
  • Fifth Ecumenical Council (Constantinople II, 553) — used the title Aeiparthenos (Ever-Virgin) for Mary, confirming established doctrine.

The New Eve

The Fathers draw a profound parallel between Eve and Mary. Eve, by her disobedience, participated in the Fall; Mary, by her free obedience (“Let it be to me according to your word,” Luke 1:38), cooperated with God in the Incarnation and thus in the salvation of the human race. Mary is the New Eve, as Christ is the New Adam.

Mary’s “yes” (fiat) was a free act, not forced. God awaited her consent. This reveals the pattern of synergy in salvation: God does not override human freedom but invites cooperation.

Fathers

  • St. Irenaeus of Lyon — the first to develop the Eve-Mary typology extensively: “The knot of Eve’s disobedience was loosened by the obedience of Mary” (Against Heresies III.22.4).
  • St. Justin Martyr — made the same comparison even earlier: “Eve, a virgin, conceived the word of the serpent and brought forth disobedience and death. But the Virgin Mary received faith and joy” (Dialogue with Trypho 100).
  • St. Ephrem the Syrian — richly developed the Eve-Mary typology in his hymns.

The Dormition (Koimesis)

The Orthodox Church celebrates the Dormition (Falling Asleep) of the Theotokos on August 15—one of the greatest feasts of the year. The tradition holds that at the end of her earthly life, Mary “fell asleep” and her body was taken up into heaven by her Son. The apostles, gathered miraculously from the ends of the earth, witnessed the empty tomb.

The Dormition is not identical to the Latin dogma of the “Assumption” (defined as de fide in 1950). Orthodoxy affirms the reality of the event but has not imposed a dogmatic definition specifying the exact manner. What is confessed is that Mary is alive in Christ—body and soul—in heaven, the first of the redeemed to share fully in the Resurrection.

Sources

  • The Liturgical Tradition — the feast of the Dormition is attested from at least the 6th century, with homilies by St. Andrew of Crete, St. John of Damascus, and St. Germanos of Constantinople.
  • St. John of Damascus — delivered three homilies on the Dormition, declaring that it was fitting for the body from which the Logos took flesh to be preserved from corruption.

Intercession of the Theotokos

The Orthodox Church invokes the Theotokos more than any other saint. She is called the greatest intercessor before her Son, the “more honourable than the Cherubim and more glorious beyond compare than the Seraphim.” Every Orthodox service includes hymns and petitions to the Theotokos.

This intercession does not diminish the unique mediatorship of Christ (1 Timothy 2:5). Mary prays to Christ for the faithful; she is not a parallel mediator but the foremost among the communion of saints who pray together with the Church on earth.

The most beloved Marian hymn in Orthodoxy, the Akathist, celebrates the Theotokos as protector, intercessor, and living icon of the Incarnation.

Fathers & Liturgical Sources

  • Sub Tuum Praesidium (3rd century) — “Beneath thy compassion we take refuge, O Theotokos. Despise not our petitions in time of trouble, but deliver us from danger, only pure, only blessed one.”
  • St. Germanos of Constantinople (d. 733) — “No one is saved except through you, O Theotokos.”
  • The Akathist Hymn (6th century) — one of the most celebrated hymns of the Orthodox Church, sung during Great Lent.

On the “Immaculate Conception”

The Orthodox Church does not accept the Roman Catholic dogma of the Immaculate Conception (defined 1854), which states that Mary was preserved from “original sin” from the moment of her conception by a special divine grace. Several objections arise from the Orthodox perspective:

  • Orthodoxy does not hold the Augustinian doctrine of inherited guilt that necessitated the dogma in the first place.
  • If Mary were exempted from the common human condition, her free consent to the Incarnation would lose its depth: she would not truly represent fallen humanity receiving salvation.
  • The Orthodox Fathers call Mary all-holy (Panagia) and all-pure (Panaghia), and they affirm that she was purified by the Holy Spirit at the Annunciation—but this is not the same as being conceived without the consequences of the Fall.

Mary is honored as the most holy, most pure, and most exalted of all created beings—but she is a creature who was sanctified and prepared by God, not one who was placed outside the common human condition from the start.
