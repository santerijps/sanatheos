Soteriology Cheat Sheet

A concise reference to the Orthodox Christian teaching on salvation, grounded in the Church Fathers and the liturgical tradition.

Salvation as Theosis

In Orthodox theology, salvation is not primarily a legal transaction but a healing and transformation of the whole human person. The ultimate goal is theosis (deification, θέωσις): real participation in the divine life, becoming “partakers of the divine nature” (2 Peter 1:4) by grace.

Theosis does not mean becoming God in essence. The creature never becomes the Creator. Rather, by the uncreated energies of God, human persons are transfigured—made radiant with divine life, restored to the likeness of God that was marred by the Fall. The distinction between God’s essence (unknowable, imparticipable) and His energies (uncreated, participable) safeguards both divine transcendence and the reality of communion.

Fathers

  • St. Athanasius of Alexandria — “God became man so that man might become god” (On the Incarnation).
  • St. Gregory Palamas — defended the essence/energies distinction at the Councils of Constantinople (1341, 1351), showing that theosis is real union with the uncreated light, not a mere metaphor.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — described theosis as the final purpose of creation: humanity is called to unite all of creation to God through love.
  • St. Irenaeus of Lyon — “The glory of God is a living man, and the life of man consists in beholding God” (Against Heresies IV.20.7).

The Fall & Ancestral Sin

Orthodoxy speaks of ancestral sin (propatorikon hamartema) rather than “original sin” in the Augustinian sense. Through Adam’s transgression, death entered the world (Romans 5:12), and all humanity inherited the consequences of the Fall: mortality, corruption, a darkened nous, and a tendency toward sin. However, humanity does not inherit Adam’s personal guilt.

The Fall is understood as a sickness, a turning away from the Source of life. Human nature became subject to death and corruption, and the nous—the organ of spiritual perception—was darkened, cutting humanity off from direct communion with God. Salvation is therefore healing: the restoration of communion with God and the reversal of death and corruption.

Fathers

  • St. John Chrysostom — interpreted Romans 5:12 as “because all sinned” (each person sins personally because of the mortality inherited from Adam), not as inherited guilt.
  • St. Athanasius — in On the Incarnation, describes the Fall as humanity sliding toward non-being, since only God is truly Being, and departure from God is departure from existence.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — distinguished the “natural will” (oriented to the good) from the “gnomic will” (deliberative, capable of error), which arises from the fallen condition.

The Work of Christ

Orthodox soteriology does not reduce the work of Christ to a single model. The Fathers employ multiple, complementary images:

Recapitulation (Anakephalaiosis)

Christ recapitulates all of human life in Himself. He passes through every stage of human existence—conception, birth, growth, temptation, suffering, death—and heals each stage from within. What Adam undid, Christ redoes and perfects. By uniting human nature to Himself, the Logos restores it to its original trajectory toward God.

Victory over Death (Christus Victor)

By dying in the flesh, Christ entered death—and because the divine Life cannot be held by death, He destroyed death from within. The Paschal hymn proclaims: “Christ is risen from the dead, trampling down death by death, and upon those in the tombs bestowing life.” The Resurrection is the central saving event: not merely proof of divinity but the actual overthrow of the power of death and Hades.

Healing & Restoration

Christ is the Physician (iatros). Sin is a sickness, death a disease. The Incarnation is the medicine: “He assumed what is ours so that He might grant us what is His” (St. Cyril). By assuming a complete human nature, the Logos heals it from within—body, soul, and nous—and makes it capable again of communion with God.

Sacrifice & Offering

Christ offers Himself as a sacrifice to the Father—but Orthodoxy insists this is not a payment to satisfy divine wrath in a juridical sense. The sacrifice is offered by the Son to the Trinity, not to appease an angry God. It is a free act of self-giving love that heals the rupture between God and humanity. Christ is simultaneously the priest, the offerer, and the offering (Liturgy of St. John Chrysostom).

Fathers

  • St. Irenaeus of Lyon — chief proponent of recapitulation theology (Against Heresies III.18–22).
  • St. Athanasius — described Christ’s death as the destruction of corruption from within (On the Incarnation 20–32).
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — rejected the idea that Christ’s blood was a “ransom paid to the devil” or a payment demanded by the Father’s justice; called it “robbery” if paid to the devil and “outrageous” if demanded by the Father (Oration 45.22).
  • St. John Chrysostom — the Paschal Homily proclaims the total victory of Christ over Hades.

Synergy: Grace & Free Will

Orthodoxy affirms synergy (synergia): salvation is a cooperation between divine grace and human freedom. God does not save anyone against their will, nor can human effort alone achieve salvation. Grace always initiates, but the human person must freely respond, cooperate, and persevere.

This is not semi-Pelagianism. Grace is absolutely primary and necessary at every step. But human freedom is real and must be exercised. The ascetic tradition of the Church—prayer, fasting, repentance, participation in the sacraments—is the arena where this synergy unfolds. Salvation is not a one-time event but a lifelong process of healing, growth, and transformation.

Orthodox theology typically speaks of salvation in three aspects: justification (being set right with God), sanctification (ongoing purification and growth in holiness), and glorification (final theosis). These are not discrete stages but overlapping dimensions of a single movement toward God.

Fathers

  • St. John Cassian — articulated the balanced view of grace and free will that became normative in the East; often mischaracterized as semi-Pelagian by Western critics, but affirmed the absolute priority of grace.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — described the movement of the human will toward God as a free response to the divine call, enabled by grace.
  • St. Mark of Ephesus — at the Council of Florence (1439), defended the Orthodox understanding of salvation against the Latin scholastic models.
  • St. Seraphim of Sarov — “The goal of the Christian life is the acquisition of the Holy Spirit”—a concise summary of theosis as lived experience.

Baptism & the Sacramental Life

Baptism is the entrance into salvation—death and resurrection with Christ (Romans 6:3–4). Through triple immersion in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, the baptized person dies to the old life and rises to new life in Christ. Baptism is followed immediately by Chrismation (anointing with holy chrism), the personal Pentecost, in which the baptized receives the seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit.

The Eucharist is the ongoing medicine of immortality (pharmakon athanasias, St. Ignatius of Antioch). In receiving the Body and Blood of Christ, the faithful are united to the risen Lord and to one another. The entire sacramental life of the Church—Confession, Anointing of the Sick, Marriage, Ordination—is the means by which grace permeates and heals every dimension of human existence.

Fathers

  • St. Cyril of Jerusalem — his Mystagogical Catecheses are the classic instruction on baptism, chrismation, and the Eucharist.
  • St. Ignatius of Antioch — called the Eucharist the “medicine of immortality, the antidote against death” (Letter to the Ephesians 20).
  • St. Nicholas Cabasilas — The Life in Christ is a comprehensive treatment of the sacramental path to theosis.

The Last Things: Death, Judgment & Resurrection

At death, the soul separates from the body and undergoes the Particular Judgment, a foretaste of its final state. The Church prays for the departed, trusting in God’s mercy.\ Orthodoxy does not teach purgatory as a place of juridical punishment but affirms that the faithful departed continue to grow and that the prayers of the living benefit them.

At the Second Coming of Christ, there will be a universal bodily resurrection and the Final Judgment. The same divine love that is paradise for the righteous is experienced as consuming fire by those who have refused it. Hell is not a place where God is absent but where His presence is experienced as torment by those who have hardened themselves against love.

The final state is the transfiguration of all creation: “a new heaven and a new earth” (Revelation 21:1), where God is “all in all” (1 Corinthians 15:28). The body is raised incorruptible and glorified, sharing in the resurrection of Christ.

Fathers

  • St. Mark of Ephesus — at Florence, defended the Orthodox rejection of purgatory while affirming the efficacy of prayer for the dead.
  • St. Isaac the Syrian — spoke of hell as the suffering of those who encounter infinite love but cannot receive it; “the scourge of love.”
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — described the final restoration as the cosmic fulfillment of God’s plan, with all creation united to God through the human person.
