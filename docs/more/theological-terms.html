Theological Terms Cheat Sheet

What these words actually mean in Orthodox Christianity—often very different from how they are popularly understood.

The Wrath of God

What people usually think it means

God gets angry at sinners and punishes them. His wrath is an emotion—fury, rage—directed at those who offend Him. He is offended, His honor is violated, and He lashes out in retribution.

What it actually means

God does not have emotions in the human sense. He is impassible (apathes)—not subject to change, mood swings, or reactive passions. When Scripture speaks of God’s “wrath,” it uses anthropomorphic language: human imagery applied to God to communicate something real about our experience of Him.

The “wrath of God” is what happens when a being that was made for communion with God turns away from Him. God’s love does not change. His light does not stop shining. But when we orient ourselves against that love, we experience it as burning, as wrath, as judgment. The same sun that warms wax hardens clay. The same fire that illumines gold burns straw.

God’s “wrath” is therefore not a change in God but a change in us—the painful consequence of encountering infinite love while clinging to sin.

How it relates to us

We are not in danger of an angry God. We are in danger of our own hardened hearts meeting an unchanging Love they refuse to receive. The ascetic life—repentance, prayer, humility—is the process of softening the heart so that what would be “wrath” becomes warmth, light, and joy.

Fathers

  • St. Isaac the Syrian — “There exists with the Creator no wrath, no fury, no jealousy, no change, no hatred, no enmity or displeasure, no suffering. In Him is no change or alternation” (Ascetical Homilies II.39).
  • St. John Chrysostom — “When you hear the word ‘wrath’ applied to God, do not imagine it to be a human passion. It is spoken of in condescension to our weakness. God acts only for our salvation.”
  • St. Anthony the Great — “God is good, dispassionate, and immutable. If anyone finds it difficult to accept that God does not change, even when those who are worthy of good things receive good from Him while those who are unworthy receive evil, such a one should understand that God sends forth His grace like the sun sends forth its rays” (Philokalia).

The Judgement of God

What people usually think it means

God holds a trial, weighs our good deeds against our bad, and renders a legal verdict: guilty or not guilty. He then sentences the guilty to eternal punishment.

What it actually means

Judgement is not primarily a courtroom scene but a revelation. The Greek word krisis (from which “judgment” comes) means a separation, a disclosure—the truth of what each person has become is made manifest in the light of Christ.

At the Last Judgement, God does not decide what we deserve, as though weighing options. Rather, He reveals what we have freely made of ourselves. Have we become people who can receive love, or people who have so hardened themselves that love is experienced as torment? The judgement is therapeutic, not retributive: it is the final stage of the healing process.

Christ’s parable of the sheep and goats (Matthew 25:31–46) shows the criterion: not doctrinal correctness or religious performance, but whether we have become people of compassion—people who recognize Christ in the hungry, the thirsty, the stranger, the prisoner.

How it relates to us

We are being “judged” right now—every choice either opens us further to God’s presence or closes us. The Final Judgement is simply the full unveiling of what each person has chosen to become.

Fathers

  • St. Maximus the Confessor — the judgement reveals the orientation of the will; those who have cultivated love find God’s presence to be paradise, while those who have cultivated selfishness find it to be fire.
  • St. Symeon the New Theologian — described the judgement as the manifestation of the light of Christ, which is joy for the pure and burning for the impure.

Salvation

What people usually think it means

A one-time event: you accept Jesus, your sins are forgiven, you get a ticket to heaven. Salvation is essentially a legal status change—from “guilty” to “not guilty”—accomplished by Christ’s death paying the penalty you owed.

What it actually means

Salvation is healing. The Greek word soteria means rescue, deliverance, making whole. It is not a legal transaction but a therapeutic process: the restoration of the whole human person—body, soul, and nous—from sickness to health, from death to life, from the image of God to the likeness of God.

Salvation has three dimensions, not one:

  • I have been saved — in Baptism, through Christ’s death and resurrection (past event).
  • I am being saved — through ongoing repentance, ascesis, the sacraments, and cooperation with grace (present process).
  • I will be saved — at the final resurrection, when the whole person (body and soul) is transfigured in theosis (future hope).

To ask “Are you saved?” in Orthodoxy is like asking “Are you healed?”—it is a journey, not a destination reached in a single moment.

How it differs from how God “saves”

God does not save us from Himself (from His own wrath). He saves us from death, corruption, and the devil, and draws us into His own life. Salvation is the gift of communion with God, not escape from God’s punishment.

Fathers

  • St. Athanasius — “God became man so that man might become god”—salvation is theosis, not merely forgiveness.
  • St. Irenaeus — Christ recapitulates all of human life, healing each part of it from within.
  • St. Seraphim of Sarov — “The goal of the Christian life is the acquisition of the Holy Spirit of God.”

Heaven & Hell

What people usually think it means

Heaven is a place “up there” where good people go. Hell is a place “down there” where bad people go, and God sends the wicked there as punishment. They are two locations, like geographical destinations.

What it actually means

Heaven and hell are not places in the spatial sense. They are experiences of the same reality: the presence of God.

For those who love God, His presence is paradise—infinite joy, light, warmth, and communion. For those who hate God or have hardened themselves against love, that same presence is experienced as torment—an unbearable burning, because love received by a soul that has refused love is agony.

God does not send anyone to hell. Hell is not a place where God is absent. Rather, hell is what happens when God’s inescapable love encounters a will that has made itself incapable of receiving it. The fire of hell and the light of heaven are the same fire: the uncreated energy of God’s love.

How it relates to us

We are not “earning” heaven or avoiding hell by our behavior. We are becoming the kind of beings who will experience God’s presence as joy or as suffering. The spiritual life is the process of transformation—of reorienting the heart toward love—so that when we stand before God, His light is warmth and not burning.

Fathers

  • St. Isaac the Syrian — “Those who are punished in Gehenna are scourged by the scourge of love. How cruel and bitter this torment of love is! It would be improper for a person to think that sinners in hell are deprived of the love of God” (Ascetical Homilies I.28).
  • St. Mark of Ephesus — rejected purgatory but affirmed that God’s mercy continues to work on the departed through the prayers of the Church.
  • Alexandre Kalomiros (20th c.) — The River of Fire popularized the patristic teaching that heaven and hell are not rewards and punishments but different experiences of the same divine love.

Sin

What people usually think it means

Sin is breaking God’s rules. It is a legal offense against a divine Lawgiver. Each sin adds to your debt, and the debt must be paid—either by you or by Christ on the Cross.

What it actually means

The Greek word for sin, hamartia (αμαρτία), literally means “missing the mark.” Sin is not primarily a legal violation but a failure to hit the target—the target being communion with God and the fulfillment of our nature as beings created in His image.

Sin is a sickness, a misdirection, a falling short of what we were made to be. It is not that God is offended by our sins the way a king is offended by a rebel. Rather, sin wounds us. It darkens the nous (the organ of spiritual perception), distorts the will, enslaves us to the passions, and separates us from the Source of life—not because God withdraws, but because we turn away.

Orthodoxy distinguishes between:

  • Ancestral sin (propatorikon hamartema) — the condition inherited from Adam: mortality, corruption, a darkened nous, and a tendency toward sin. We inherit the consequences, not Adam’s personal guilt.
  • Personal sin — our own choices to miss the mark, which deepen the wound and further enslave us to the passions.

How it relates to us

Repentance (metanoia, “change of mind”) is not groveling before an angry judge. It is turning around—reorienting the whole self back toward God, the Physician, for healing. Confession is not a legal pardon; it is part of the therapeutic process.

Fathers

  • St. John Chrysostom — taught that we inherit mortality from Adam, not guilt; each person sins because mortality and corruption weigh upon human nature.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — described sin as the misuse of the natural powers of the soul: desire (epithymia) and anger (thymos) were created good but are misdirected by the fallen will.
  • St. John Climacus — The Ladder of Divine Ascent maps the path from the passions (sin’s grip on the soul) to apatheia (freedom from the passions, not indifference).

Righteousness

What people usually think it means

Righteousness is a legal status: God declares you “righteous” (justified), meaning He treats you as if you were sinless, even though inwardly you remain a sinner. Christ’s righteousness is “imputed” to you as a credit on your account.

What it actually means

The Greek word dikaiosyne (δικαιοσύνη) means not only juridical “justice” but fundamentally rightness, integrity, being rightly ordered. To be righteous is to be actually healed—to have one’s whole being rightly oriented toward God. It is not a legal fiction but a real transformation.

God’s righteousness is not His demand for legal satisfaction. It is His faithfulness—His commitment to set right what has gone wrong, to heal what is sick, to restore what is fallen. When Scripture says “God is righteous,” it means God is true to His nature, which is love, and He acts to make all things whole.

Human righteousness is participation in divine righteousness—it is the fruit of theosis, the gradual transformation of the person into the likeness of God through the cooperation of grace and free will. It is not imputed (credited from outside) but imparted (communicated and received).

How it differs from God’s righteousness

God’s righteousness is His own uncreated, eternal, unchanging nature. Our righteousness is always received, always participated, always a gift—we are righteous by grace, not by nature. Even the most advanced saint is righteous only because God’s life dwells within them.

Fathers

  • St. Maximus the Confessor — righteousness is the right ordering of all the powers of the soul toward God; the intellect knows truth, the desire loves the good, the anger repels evil.
  • St. Gregory Palamas — righteousness is a participation in the uncreated energy of God, not a legal declaration.
  • St. Mark the Ascetic — “God’s justice is not like human justice. God’s justice is mercy” (On Those Who Think They Are Made Righteous by Works).

Saint

What people usually think it means

Either (a) a morally perfect person, a superhero of virtue far removed from ordinary people, or (b) in some Protestant traditions, every Christian is automatically a “saint” by virtue of being “declared righteous”—the word has no special content beyond “believer.”

What it actually means

The Greek word hagios (αʼγιος) means “holy,” and holiness means being set apart for God—being filled with the uncreated grace of the Holy Spirit. A saint is a person in whom theosis has visibly taken hold: the divine life shines through them. They are not perfect in the sense of never sinning, but they have been so deeply healed and transformed by grace that they become transparent to God’s light.

All Christians are called to be saints (1 Corinthians 1:2). The saints whom the Church formally glorifies (canonizes) are those whose holiness has been recognized by the community of the faithful—usually through miracles, the incorruption of their relics, their witness in life and death, and the liturgical sense of the Church guided by the Holy Spirit.

How saints relate to us

The saints are not dead. They are alive in Christ, more alive than we are, and they pray for us. The Church is one body spanning heaven and earth, and the saints are its most radiant members. When we venerate the saints, we do not worship them—we honor what God has accomplished in them and ask for their intercessions.

The saints are also proof that theosis is real. They show that human nature—our nature—is capable of being transfigured by grace. Every saint is evidence that the Gospel works.

How sainthood differs from God’s holiness

God is holy by nature—He is the Source of all holiness. The saints are holy by participation in God’s uncreated energies. Like iron made red-hot in a fire, the saint glows with a light that is not its own but truly belongs to it while it remains in the fire.

Fathers

  • St. John of Damascus — defended the veneration of saints and their relics, arguing that the bodies of the saints are temples of the Holy Spirit and channels of grace.
  • St. Basil the Great — “Through the Spirit we acquire a likeness to God; and what is more than anyone could hope for, we become gods.”
  • St. Seraphim of Sarov — a living example of theosis; in his conversation with Motovilov, his face shone with the uncreated light, demonstrating what every Christian is called to become.
  • St. Athanasius — in his Life of Anthony, showed the prototype of Christian sainthood: a life of ascesis, prayer, and spiritual warfare leading to transfiguration.

Summary: The Therapeutic Model

The common thread running through all these terms is the therapeutic model of Orthodox theology. The Church is a hospital, not a courtroom. Christ is the Physician, not a prosecuting attorney. The goal is healing, not acquittal:
