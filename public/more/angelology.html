Angelology & Demonology

A concise reference to the Orthodox Christian teaching on the angelic hosts, the fallen angels, and the spiritual warfare of the Christian life.

The Nature of Angels

Angels (angeloi, αʼγγελοι, “messengers”) are bodiless rational beings—created persons with intellect and free will, but without material bodies. They were created before the material world, and their purpose is to serve God, worship Him, and act as His messengers and ministers in creation.

Angels are not omnipresent, not omniscient, and not omnipotent. They are creatures, not gods. They surpass human beings in power and knowledge, but they are finite and limited. They do not know the future (except what God reveals to them), and they cannot read human thoughts directly.

Though bodiless, angels are not absolutely immaterial in the way God is. The Fathers teach that only God is truly immaterial. Angels have a kind of subtle materiality—they are bound to place (they cannot be in two places at once) and they move from one location to another, though with a speed incomparably greater than any physical creature.

Angels were created with free will, but having made their choice for or against God at the beginning, their will is now fixed. The good angels are confirmed in goodness and can no longer fall. The fallen angels are confirmed in evil and can no longer repent.

Fathers

  • St. John of Damascus — “An angel is an intellectual substance, ever in motion, endowed with free will, incorporeal, serving God, having by grace obtained an immortal nature” (An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith II.3).
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — described the angels as “secondary lights” reflecting the First Light, created to minister and to praise.
  • St. Basil the Great — taught that the angels were created before the material world and confirmed in holiness by the Holy Spirit.

The Angelic Hierarchy

The Orthodox tradition, following Dionysius the Areopagite (The Celestial Hierarchy), organizes the angels into nine orders (tagmata) arranged in three triads. Each triad receives divine illumination and passes it down to the next, so that the light of God flows through the entire hierarchy like light through progressively clearer glass:

First Triad — Closest to God

  • Seraphim (Seraphim, “the burning ones”) — stand closest to the throne of God, ablaze with love. They cry “Holy, Holy, Holy” (Isaiah 6:2–3). They are consumed with the fire of divine love and communicate this fire to the orders below.
  • Cherubim (Cherubim, “fullness of knowledge”) — possess the deepest knowledge of God among created beings. They guard the throne of God (Genesis 3:24; Ezekiel 1, 10). They illumine the lower orders with divine wisdom.
  • Thrones (Thronoi) — bear the presence of God and serve as the seat of His glory. Through them, God’s justice and authority are communicated downward.

Second Triad — The Governors

  • Dominions (Kyriotetes) — regulate the duties of the lower angels and govern the ordering of the cosmos.
  • Virtues (Dynameis, “powers”) — ministers of God’s miracles and signs. They strengthen human beings for spiritual struggle.
  • Powers (Exousiai, “authorities”) — restrain the power of demons and protect the cosmos from being overwhelmed by evil.

Third Triad — Closest to Humanity

  • Principalities (Archai) — assigned to nations, peoples, and regions. They guard kingdoms and communities.
  • Archangels (Archangeloi) — bring the most important messages from God to human beings. They lead in spiritual warfare.
  • Angels (Angeloi) — the order closest to human beings. Each person has a guardian angel assigned from baptism (or from birth, according to some Fathers), who protects, guides, and intercedes.

The Named Archangels

Orthodox tradition names seven archangels, though only three are named in Scripture:

  • Michael (“Who is like God?”) — commander of the heavenly hosts, protector of Israel and the Church, the one who cast Satan from heaven (Revelation 12:7–9; Daniel 10:13, 12:1; Jude 9).
  • Gabriel (“Strength of God”) — the announcer, who brought the news of the Incarnation to the Theotokos (Luke 1:26–38) and appeared to Daniel (Daniel 8:16, 9:21) and Zechariah (Luke 1:19).
  • Raphael (“Healing of God”) — the healer, who accompanied Tobias (Tobit 3:17, 12:15).
  • Uriel (“Light/Fire of God”) — associated with enlightenment (2 Esdras 4:1).
  • Selaphiel (“Prayer of God”) — the angel of prayer and intercession.
  • Jegudiel (“Praise of God”) — patron of those who labor for God’s glory.
  • Barachiel (“Blessing of God”) — bearer of God’s blessings.

Sources

  • Dionysius the Areopagite — The Celestial Hierarchy is the foundational text for the ninefold ordering.
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — described the angelic orders in Oration 28.
  • St. John of Damascus — systematized the teaching in An Exact Exposition II.3.

Guardian Angels

Every baptized Christian (and, according to many Fathers, every human being) has a guardian angel—a personal angelic companion assigned by God to watch over, protect, guide, and intercede for that person throughout their life.

The guardian angel does not override free will. He suggests the good, warns against evil, and prays for the person, but he cannot force obedience. When we sin, the guardian angel grieves; when we repent, he rejoices. He accompanies the soul after death and acts as an advocate.

“See that you do not despise one of these little ones. For I tell you that in heaven their angels always see the face of my Father who is in heaven” (Matthew 18:10).

Fathers

  • St. Basil the Great — “Beside each believer stands an angel as protector and shepherd, leading him to life” (Against Eunomius III.1).
  • St. Macarius the Great — described the guardian angel as a friend and companion who never abandons the person unless they persist in driving him away through habitual sin.

The Fall of the Angels

Before the creation of the material world, a portion of the angels, led by one of the highest among them, freely chose to reject God. This is the fall of Satan (Satanas, “the adversary”) and his followers. The cause of the fall was pride—the desire to be like God on one’s own terms, to be the center of existence rather than to serve the Creator.

The fallen angels became demons (daimones). They did not lose their angelic nature—they are still powerful, intelligent, and spiritual beings—but their nature is now perverted, twisted by malice and envy. They retain their powers but use them only for destruction.

The fall of the angels was irrevocable. Unlike human beings, who sin partly because of the weakness of the flesh and the inheritance of mortality, the angels sinned with full knowledge and full freedom—purely spiritual beings making a purely spiritual choice. There is no repentance for them because their will is fixed: they chose against God with the totality of their being.

Fathers

  • St. John of Damascus — “He who from among the angelic powers was set over the earthly realm, and to whom God committed the guardianship of the earth, was not made wicked by nature but was good, and came into being for good; but by his own free choice he turned from what was according to nature to what was contrary to nature” (Exact Exposition II.4).
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — described Satan’s fall as the first misuse of freedom: the angel of light became darkness by turning from the Source of light.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — the demons are trapped in their own self-will; they cannot repent because they will not, and they will not because their nature, once turned, is fixed.

The Nature & Tactics of Demons

Demons are not equal and opposite to God. They are creatures—powerful, but infinitely less powerful than their Creator. The devil is not the “evil god” in a cosmic dualism; he is a fallen creature who can do nothing without God’s permission (cf. Job 1:12).

What demons can do

  • Tempt — they suggest sinful thoughts (logismoi) to the mind. They cannot force sin; they can only propose. The Fathers call this the “first movement” (prosbole), and it is not itself a sin.
  • Deceive — they present evil as good, distort perception, and create illusions. The devil is “the father of lies” (John 8:44).
  • Oppress — they can afflict the body and the emotions from the outside, creating fear, despair, confusion, or physical illness.
  • Possess (in extreme cases) — inhabit and control a person’s body, though not their soul or free will in its deepest sense. Possession occurs with God’s permission and can be remedied through exorcism, prayer, and the sacraments.

What demons cannot do

  • They cannot force anyone to sin.
  • They cannot read human thoughts directly (but they can observe behavior and make shrewd inferences).
  • They cannot be in two places at once.
  • They cannot overpower God, the Theotokos, or the saints.
  • They cannot act without divine permission.

The Eight Passions

The Fathers identify eight principal passions (logismoi) through which demons attack the human soul. These are not “sins” in themselves but disordered movements of the soul that demons exploit and inflame:

  1. Gluttony (gastrimargia) — disordered desire for food and drink.
  2. Lust (porneia) — disordered sexual desire.
  3. Avarice (philargyria) — love of money and possessions.
  4. Sadness (lype) — dejection, self-pity, worldly grief.
  5. Anger (orge) — wrath directed at others.
  6. Acedia (akedia) — spiritual listlessness, the “noonday demon.”
  7. Vainglory (kenodoxia) — desire for human praise and recognition.
  8. Pride (hyperephania) — the root of all passions; self-elevation above God and neighbor.

Fathers

  • Evagrius Ponticus — the first to systematize the eight logismoi (later adapted by St. John Cassian and eventually condensed into the Western “seven deadly sins”).
  • St. John Cassian — transmitted Evagrius’s scheme to the West in his Conferences and Institutes.
  • St. John Climacus — The Ladder of Divine Ascent maps each passion and its remedy in the spiritual life.
  • St. Athanasius — in the Life of Anthony, described the desert saint’s encounters with demons in vivid detail, showing both their power and their limitations.

Spiritual Warfare

The Christian life is a battle—not against flesh and blood “but against principalities, against powers, against the rulers of the darkness of this age, against spiritual hosts of wickedness in the heavenly places” (Ephesians 6:12). The Orthodox tradition takes this literally: the spiritual life is a real warfare against real, personal, intelligent evil beings.

The Stages of Temptation

The Fathers map the progression of a demonic attack on the soul:

  1. Provocation (prosbole) — a thought is suggested to the mind. This is not a sin; it happens to everyone, even to Christ in the desert.
  2. Dialogue (syndiasmos) — the person entertains the thought, “talks” with it. This is where danger begins.
  3. Consent (synkatathesis) — the will assents to the thought. This is sin.
  4. Captivity (aichmalosia) — the mind is taken captive; the person is compelled by the passion.
  5. Passion (pathos) — the sin becomes habitual, a deep-rooted disposition of the soul.

Weapons of the Christian

  • The Jesus Prayer — “Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner.” The continuous invocation of the Name of Christ disperses demonic thoughts.
  • The Sign of the Cross — demons fear the Cross; the saints testify that making the sign of the Cross drives away temptation.
  • The Sacraments — especially Confession (which breaks the hold of hidden sins) and the Eucharist (which unites us to Christ).
  • Fasting — weakens the body’s grip on the soul, making the mind clearer and more resistant to suggestion.
  • Watchfulness (nepsis) — the disciplined guarding of the mind against intrusive thoughts. The core practice of Hesychasm.
  • Humility — the ultimate weapon, because pride is the root of all demonic power. The humble person has nothing the devil can grasp.
  • Obedience — to a spiritual father, to the Church, to the commandments—this cuts off self-will, which is the entry point for demonic deception.

Fathers

  • St. Anthony the Great — the prototype of the spiritual warrior; the Life of Anthony by St. Athanasius is the foundational text on demonic combat.
  • St. John Climacus — The Ladder is a step-by-step guide to overcoming the passions and ascending to God.
  • St. Hesychios the Priest — in On Watchfulness and Holiness (in the Philokalia), described the practice of nepsis in detail.
  • St. Paisios of Mount Athos (1924–1994) — in his Spiritual Counsels, gave practical, accessible teaching on dealing with demonic temptation in daily life.

Exorcism

Exorcism is a normal part of Orthodox life, not an extraordinary spectacle. Every catechumen undergoes exorcism prayers before baptism—renouncing Satan and all his works. The baptismal rite includes the command: “The Lord rebukes you, O Devil!”

In cases of severe demonic oppression or possession, the Church has a rite of special exorcism, performed by a priest (usually with the bishop’s blessing) through prayer, the reading of Scripture, the invocation of the Name of Christ, and the Sign of the Cross. Exorcism is not magic; it is the authority of Christ exercised through His Church.

The key principle: demons are already defeated. Christ destroyed the power of the devil through His death and resurrection (“trampling down death by death”). The demons fight on, but they fight a lost war. Every exorcism is a manifestation of the victory already won by Christ on the Cross and in the Harrowing of Hades.

Fathers

  • St. Cyril of Jerusalem — described the baptismal exorcism in his Catechetical Lectures.
  • St. John Chrysostom — taught that the Name of Jesus is sufficient to expel any demon, because Christ has already conquered them.

The Toll-Houses (Telonia)

A widespread tradition in Orthodox piety describes the soul after death passing through a series of aerial “toll-houses” (telonia), where demons challenge the soul by presenting its sins. At each station, the guardian angel and the demons contend for the soul. If the soul has repented and been cleansed, it passes through; if its sins are unrepented, the demons claim it.

This teaching is not a formal dogma and has been debated within Orthodoxy. Some Fathers endorse it (St. Cyril of Alexandria, Theophylact of Ohrid); others are cautious. The essential point it conveys is not its exact details but the reality that the particular judgment after death is real, that the spiritual state of the soul at death matters, that demons are real adversaries, and that the prayers of the Church for the departed are of critical importance.

Sources

  • The Life of St. Basil the New (10th century) — the vision of Theodora’s passage through the toll-houses is the most detailed account in Orthodox literature.
  • St. Cyril of Alexandria — “At our departure, the princes of the air come to us, and they examine and question us. And if they find us owing them anything, they seize us and hold us.”
  • St. Ignatius Brianchaninov (19th century) — defended the toll-house tradition as consistent with patristic teaching.

Key Patristic Axioms

“Beside each believer stands an angel as protector and shepherd, leading him to life.”
— St. Basil the Great, Against Eunomius III.1
“Demons are crafty. They pretend to yield so that they can launch a worse attack.”
— St. Anthony the Great (from St. Athanasius, Life of Anthony)
“The whole of the spiritual life can be reduced to this: watch your thoughts.”
— Abba Poemen the Great
“Where humility is, there the devil cannot even approach.”
— St. John Climacus, The Ladder of Divine Ascent
