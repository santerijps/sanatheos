Christology Cheat Sheet

A concise reference to the Orthodox Christian teaching on the Person of Christ, grounded in the Seven Ecumenical Councils and the Church Fathers.

The Holy Trinity

God is one in essence (ousia) and three in persons (hypostases): the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. The three Persons share the same divine nature fully and equally. There is no division of the essence, no subordination of being.

The Father is unbegotten (agennetos), the sole source and cause (aitia) of the Godhead. The Son is eternally begotten (gennetos) of the Father—not made, not created, but begotten before all ages. The Holy Spirit eternally proceeds (ekporeusis) from the Father alone.

Each Person fully possesses the one divine nature. The distinction is in their mode of origin (the personal or hypostatic properties), not in what they are.

Councils & Fathers

  • First Ecumenical Council (Nicaea I, 325) — confessed the Son as homoousios (consubstantial) with the Father, against Arianism.
  • Second Ecumenical Council (Constantinople I, 381) — affirmed the full divinity of the Holy Spirit and expanded the Nicene Creed.
  • The Cappadocian Fathers (Sts. Basil the Great, Gregory the Theologian, Gregory of Nyssa) — distinguished ousia (essence) from hypostasis (person) and articulated the one essence / three hypostases formula.
  • St. Athanasius of Alexandria — champion of Nicaea; insisted that if the Son is not fully God, salvation is impossible, because only God can deify.

The Incarnation

The Second Person of the Trinity, the eternal Logos, became man without ceasing to be God. He assumed a complete human nature—body, soul, and nous (intellect)—from the Virgin Mary by the Holy Spirit. The Incarnation is not a mixing of natures, nor a conversion of divinity into humanity, but the assumption of humanity into the Person of the Logos.

The Virgin Mary is rightly called Theotokos (God-bearer, Θεοτόκος), because she bore not a mere man but the incarnate God—one Person who is both divine and human.

Councils & Fathers

  • Third Ecumenical Council (Ephesus, 431) — affirmed the title Theotokos and condemned Nestorius, who divided Christ into two subjects.
  • St. Cyril of Alexandria — defended the hypostatic union: one incarnate nature (person) of the God-Logos, inseparably united to a complete humanity.
  • St. Athanasius — “God became man so that man might become god” (On the Incarnation).

The Two Natures of Christ

Christ is one Person (hypostasis) in two natures (physeis): divine and human. These two natures are united in the single hypostasis of the Logos. The union is:

  • Without confusion (asynchytos) — the natures are not mixed into a third thing.
  • Without change (atreptos) — neither nature is altered or diminished.
  • Without division (adiairetos) — the natures are never separated into two persons.
  • Without separation (achoristos) — the union is permanent and indissoluble.

Christ’s humanity is complete: a rational soul, a human body, a human intellect (nous). It is not a truncated or partial humanity. To deny any element of Christ’s humanity is to leave that element unredeemed—“what is not assumed is not healed” (St. Gregory the Theologian).

Councils & Fathers

  • Fourth Ecumenical Council (Chalcedon, 451) — defined the four negative adverbs (asynchytos, atreptos, adiairetos, achoristos) and affirmed one Christ in two natures.
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — “That which is not assumed is not healed” (Epistle 101), against Apollinarianism which denied Christ a human nous.
  • St. Leo the Great — his Tome (449) articulated the two natures acting in communion: “each nature performs what is proper to it in communion with the other.”

The Hypostatic Union

The hypostatic union means that the two natures are united in the one Person (hypostasis) of the eternal Logos. The Logos is the single subject of all of Christ’s actions and experiences—both divine and human. There are not two “he’s” in Christ but one “He” who acts in and through both natures.

Because of this union, there is a communication of properties (communicatio idiomatum): what belongs to one nature can be predicated of the one Person. Thus we can truly say “God suffered in the flesh” and “the man Christ is worshipped,” not because the divine nature suffered or the human nature is God, but because the one Person who is God experienced suffering in His human nature.

Councils & Fathers

  • Fifth Ecumenical Council (Constantinople II, 553) — clarified the hypostatic union, affirming that the one hypostasis of the Logos is the subject of both natures; anathematized those who posited two persons or subjects in Christ.
  • St. Cyril of Alexandria — central architect of the concept of hypostatic union; his Twelve Anathemas and letters to Nestorius are foundational.
  • St. John of Damascus — systematized Christology in An Exact Exposition of the Orthodox Faith, explaining the union without confusion and the enhypostaton: Christ’s human nature has its personal existence in the hypostasis of the Logos, not as a separate person.

Two Wills & Two Energies

Because Christ has two complete natures, He has two natural wills (thelemata) and two natural energies/operations (energeiai): one divine and one human. Will and energy belong to nature, not to person. To deny a human will in Christ is to deny a complete human nature.

Natural Will (thelema physikon)

The natural will is the volitional capacity that belongs to a nature as such. Every complete human nature wills; every divine nature wills. Christ possesses both. His human will is not autonomous or opposed to the divine will, but freely and perfectly follows it in loving obedience. In Gethsemane, Christ’s human will naturally shrank from death (“let this cup pass from me”), yet freely submitted to the divine will (“not my will, but yours be done”).

Gnomic Will (thelema gnomikon)

The gnomic will (from gnome, disposition or deliberative inclination) refers to the mode of willing that involves deliberation, uncertainty, and the possibility of choosing between good and evil. It is characteristic of fallen or created persons who do not have an unwavering orientation toward the Good.

Christ does not have a gnomic will. Because the Person who wills humanly in Christ is the divine Logos, His human will is perfectly and unfailingly oriented toward the Good. There is no inner deliberation between good and evil, no wavering. This is not because His humanity is forced, but because in the hypostatic union it is the Logos Himself who wills humanly, and the Logos cannot will evil.

This distinction is critical for soteriology: Christ’s sinlessness is not merely moral achievement but a consequence of who He is—the divine Person willing through a complete but non-gnomic human will.

Two Energies

Likewise, Christ has two natural energies (operations). He works miracles by divine energy and hungers, weeps, and dies by human energy. These are not two separate agents acting in parallel, but one Person acting through two natures, each nature contributing its proper operation in communion with the other.

Councils & Fathers

  • Sixth Ecumenical Council (Constantinople III, 680–681) — condemned Monothelitism and Monoenergism; defined that Christ has two natural wills and two natural energies, the human will being subject to and in harmony with the divine will, yet never destroyed or absorbed.
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — the chief theologian of the two-wills doctrine. Developed the distinction between natural will and gnomic will; argued that to deny a human will in Christ is to deny a genuine Incarnation and destroy the possibility of human salvation. He suffered exile and mutilation for this teaching.
  • St. John of Damascus — synthesized Maximian Christology: two wills, two energies, the human will deified but not abolished.

Body, Soul, Spirit & Nous

Eastern Orthodox anthropology and Christology affirm that Christ assumed a complete human nature. The Fathers describe the human composite in several complementary ways:

Body (soma)

Christ took a real, physical, material body from the Virgin Mary. He was not a phantom or an apparition (against Docetism). He experienced hunger, thirst, fatigue, pain, and death in His body. After the Resurrection, His body is glorified yet remains truly corporeal (“handle me and see, for a spirit does not have flesh and bones as you see I have,” Luke 24:39).

Soul (psyche)

Christ has a rational human soul (—not merely an animating life-force but a soul capable of thought, emotion, and volition. He experienced genuine human sorrow, anguish, and joy. The soul is the locus of the human will and human affections in Christ.

Spirit / Nous (pneuma / nous)

In the patristic tradition, nous (often rendered “intellect” or “mind”) is the highest faculty of the soul—the organ of spiritual perception, the capacity by which a human being knows God. Some Fathers speak of a tripartite anthropology: body, soul, and spirit/nous (following 1 Thess 5:23).

Christ possesses a human nous. This was the point of contention with Apollinaris of Laodicea (c. 310–390), who taught that the divine Logos replaced the human nous in Christ. The Fathers categorically rejected this: if Christ has no human mind, the human mind is not healed. The Logos assumed a human intellect in order to redeem human intellect.

Councils & Fathers

  • First Council of Constantinople (381) — condemned Apollinarianism; affirmed that Christ has a complete humanity including a rational soul/nous.
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — formulated the axiom “the unassumed is the unhealed” precisely against Apollinaris’s denial of the human nous in Christ.
  • St. John of Damascus — affirmed body, soul, and nous in Christ’s humanity and described how each is deified through the union.

Humanity & Divinity in Union

Christ’s humanity, while complete and genuine, is not “independent.” It has its personal existence (enhypostaton) in the hypostasis of the Logos. It was never a separate human person. From the first moment of conception, the human nature existed in the divine Person.

Through the hypostatic union, Christ’s humanity is deified—permeated by divine grace and glory—without being destroyed or absorbed. The humanity remains fully human; the divinity remains fully divine. This is the pattern and promise for all human salvation: theosis (deification) is participation in the divine nature (2 Peter 1:4) by grace, not by confusion of essence.

The Distinction of Essence & Energies

Orthodox theology (following St. Gregory Palamas and the Palamite Councils of the 14th century) distinguishes between God’s essence (ousia), which is utterly unknowable and imparticipable, and God’s energies (energeiai), which are the uncreated, eternal operations of God through which He truly communicates Himself to creation. Deification is real participation in the uncreated energies of God, not in the divine essence.

In Christ, the divine energies permeate the human nature, making it radiant with divine life (as shown on Mount Tabor at the Transfiguration, where the uncreated light shone through His body). This is the prototype of what all the saints are called to share by grace.

Councils & Fathers

  • St. Gregory Palamas (1296–1359) — articulated the essence/energies distinction to defend the reality of theosis and the experience of the uncreated light. Vindicated at the Councils of Constantinople (1341, 1351).
  • St. Maximus the Confessor — described the Logos as the unifying principle of all creation; theosis is the ultimate purpose of the Incarnation.
  • St. John of Damascus — explained the perichoresis (mutual interpenetration) of the two natures in Christ: the divinity permeates the humanity (like fire permeates iron, making it glow), but the humanity does not permeate the divinity.

The Seven Ecumenical Councils — Summary

Council Year Key Christological Definition
Nicaea I 325 The Son is homoousios (consubstantial) with the Father—fully God. Condemned Arianism.
Constantinople I 381 Divinity of the Holy Spirit. Complete humanity of Christ (against Apollinarianism). Expanded Creed.
Ephesus 431 Mary is Theotokos. One Person in Christ (against Nestorianism).
Chalcedon 451 Two natures, one hypostasis. United without confusion, change, division, or separation.
Constantinople II 553 Clarified hypostatic union. The Person of the Logos is the single subject of both natures.
Constantinople III 680–681 Two natural wills and two natural energies in Christ. Condemned Monothelitism.
Nicaea II 787 Veneration of icons affirmed—grounded in the reality of the Incarnation: because the Logos truly became visible flesh, He can be depicted.

Key Patristic Axioms

“God became man so that man might become god.”
— St. Athanasius of Alexandria, On the Incarnation
“That which is not assumed is not healed; but that which is united to God is also saved.”
— St. Gregory the Theologian, Epistle 101
“The Word of God, who is God, wills to effect the mystery of His incarnation always and in all things.”
— St. Maximus the Confessor, Ambiguum 7
“I do not worship matter, I worship the Creator of matter, who became matter for me, who took up His abode in matter, and who, through matter, effected my salvation.”
— St. John of Damascus, On the Divine Images
