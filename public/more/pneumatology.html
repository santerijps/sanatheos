Pneumatology Cheat Sheet

A concise reference to the Orthodox Christian teaching on the Holy Spirit—His Person, procession, and work in the Church and in creation.

The Person of the Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit is the Third Person of the Holy Trinity—fully God, consubstantial (homoousios) with the Father and the Son. He is not a force, an energy, or an impersonal power but a divine Person (hypostasis), possessing the fullness of the one divine nature.

The Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed confesses the Spirit as “the Lord, the Giver of Life, who proceeds from the Father, who with the Father and the Son together is worshipped and glorified, who spoke through the prophets.”

The Spirit is called “Lord” (Kyrios) because He shares the divine lordship. He is “Giver of Life” (Zoopoion) because all life—biological, spiritual, and eternal—flows from Him. He is worshipped and glorified together with the Father and the Son, not as a lesser deity.

Councils & Fathers

  • Second Ecumenical Council (Constantinople I, 381) — affirmed the full divinity of the Holy Spirit and expanded the Creed to include the article on the Spirit, against the Pneumatomachians (“Spirit-fighters”) who denied His divinity.
  • St. Basil the Great — On the Holy Spirit is the foundational patristic treatise, demonstrating from Scripture and liturgical practice that the Spirit receives the same honor and worship as the Father and the Son.
  • St. Gregory the Theologian — called the Spirit “God” directly and argued for a progressive revelation of the Trinity: the Father revealed in the Old Testament, the Son in the New, the Spirit in the age of the Church (Oration 31).
  • St. Athanasius of Alexandria — Letters to Serapion defended the Spirit’s divinity: if the Spirit is not God, then baptism in His name cannot sanctify.

The Procession of the Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit eternally proceeds from the Father alone (ek monou tou Patros). This is the hypostatic property (personal characteristic) of the Spirit, just as “unbegottenness” is the property of the Father and “begottenness” is the property of the Son.

The Father is the sole cause (aitia) and source (pege) of both the Son and the Spirit. The Son is begotten; the Spirit proceeds. These are two distinct modes of origination from the one Source, the Father. This monarchy of the Father preserves the unity of the Godhead: there is one God because there is one Father, one Source.

The Filioque Controversy

The Latin Church added “and the Son” (Filioque) to the Creed, so that it reads “who proceeds from the Father and the Son.” This addition, made unilaterally without an Ecumenical Council, is rejected by the Orthodox Church on both procedural and theological grounds:

  • Procedural — the Creed was established by the Second and confirmed by subsequent Ecumenical Councils. No local church has the authority to alter it unilaterally. The Third Ecumenical Council (Ephesus, 431) explicitly forbade additions to the Creed.
  • Theological — the Filioque introduces a second cause into the Trinity, undermining the monarchy of the Father. If the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, the Father is no longer the sole source of divinity. This tends to blur the distinction of Persons or subordinate the Spirit to both the Father and the Son.

Orthodoxy does acknowledge that the Spirit is sent through the Son in the economy (God’s action in history)—as in John 15:26, “whom I will send to you from the Father.” But the eternal procession (the Spirit’s mode of existence within the Trinity) is from the Father alone.

Councils & Fathers

  • Second Ecumenical Council (381) — established the Creed with “who proceeds from the Father” (John 15:26), without any mention of the Son in the eternal procession.
  • Third Ecumenical Council (Ephesus, 431) — Canon 7 forbade any alteration of the Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed.
  • St. Photios the Great (c. 810–893) — The Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit is the classic Orthodox refutation of the Filioque, demonstrating its theological incoherence.
  • St. Gregory Palamas — further refined the distinction between the eternal procession (from the Father alone) and the temporal mission (sent through the Son).
  • St. Mark of Ephesus — defended the Orthodox position against the Filioque at the Council of Florence (1439), refusing to sign the union decree.

The Holy Spirit in Creation

The Spirit is the “Giver of Life” not only in the order of redemption but in creation itself. “The Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters” (Genesis 1:2). All life, all beauty, all order in creation is sustained by the Spirit. The Psalmist says: “When you send forth your Spirit, they are created; and you renew the face of the ground” (Psalm 104:30).

The Fathers describe the Trinitarian pattern of creation: the Father creates through the Son in the Holy Spirit. The Son is the Logos through whom all things are made (John 1:3); the Spirit is the one who gives life and perfects what is created. Nothing exists apart from the sustaining presence of the Spirit.

Fathers

  • St. Basil the Great — described the Spirit’s role in creation as “perfecting” or “completing” what the Father initiates through the Son (On the Holy Spirit 16.38).
  • St. Irenaeus of Lyon — called the Son and the Spirit “the two hands of the Father” in creation (Against Heresies V.6.1).

Pentecost & the Church

Pentecost (Acts 2) is the birthday of the Church. The Holy Spirit descends upon the apostles, constituting them as the Body of Christ. From this moment, the Church is a Spirit-filled community—not merely a human organization but the temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 3:16).

The Spirit bestows the charismata (gifts): prophecy, teaching, healing, tongues, discernment, and above all love (1 Corinthians 12–13). He guides the Church into “all truth” (John 16:13), preserving her from error through the councils, the Fathers, and the living Tradition.

Each baptized Christian receives a personal Pentecost in the sacrament of Chrismation, sealed with the words: “The seal of the gift of the Holy Spirit.” The entire sacramental life of the Church is the work of the Spirit: it is the Spirit who transforms bread and wine into the Body and Blood of Christ in the Epiclesis (invocation of the Spirit) of the Divine Liturgy.

Fathers

  • St. John Chrysostom — preached extensively on Pentecost and the gifts of the Spirit.
  • St. Cyril of Jerusalem — in the Catechetical Lectures, taught the catechumens about the work of the Spirit in baptism and chrismation.
  • St. Symeon the New Theologian (949–1022) — insisted that the experience of the Holy Spirit is not reserved for the apostolic age but is the normal Christian life; every believer should consciously experience the Spirit’s presence.

The Holy Spirit & Theosis

The Spirit is the agent of theosis (deification). It is through the Spirit that human persons participate in the uncreated divine energies and are transfigured into the likeness of God. “We all, with unveiled face, beholding as in a mirror the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from glory to glory, just as by the Spirit of the Lord” (2 Corinthians 3:18).

The uncreated light seen by the apostles on Mount Tabor, and experienced by the saints in prayer, is the light of the Holy Spirit—the divine energy. St. Gregory Palamas defended the reality of this experience: the saints truly see and participate in God’s own light, which is not created or symbolic but the uncreated glory of the Trinity.

The goal of the Christian life, as St. Seraphim of Sarov taught, is “the acquisition of the Holy Spirit.” Every prayer, every fast, every act of love is a means to this end: opening oneself to the transforming presence of the Spirit.

Fathers

  • St. Gregory Palamas — the essence/energies distinction safeguards the reality of theosis through the Spirit: we participate in God’s uncreated energies, not His unknowable essence.
  • St. Seraphim of Sarov (1754–1833) — in his conversation with Motovilov, demonstrated the visible transfiguration of a person filled with the Holy Spirit.
  • St. Symeon the New Theologian — described his own experience of the divine light and insisted that such experience is the birthright of every Christian.
  • St. Basil the Great — “Through the Spirit we become citizens of heaven, we are admitted into the company of the angels, we enter into eternal happiness, we abide in God, and by the Spirit we acquire a likeness to God” (On the Holy Spirit 9.23).

The Prayer to the Holy Spirit

The most fundamental Orthodox prayer to the Holy Spirit, recited at the beginning of every service and personal prayer rule:

“O Heavenly King, the Comforter, the Spirit of Truth, who art everywhere present and fillest all things, Treasury of blessings and Giver of Life: come and abide in us, and cleanse us from every impurity, and save our souls, O Good One.”
— Prayer to the Holy Spirit

This prayer confesses the Spirit as omnipresent (“everywhere present and fillest all things”), as the source of all good (“Treasury of blessings”), as Giver of Life, and as the one who sanctifies (“cleanse us from every impurity”). It is an invitation: the Spirit does not force His way in but comes when He is asked.

Key Patristic Axioms

“Through the Spirit we acquire a likeness to God; and what is more than anyone could hope for, we become gods.”
— St. Basil the Great, On the Holy Spirit 9.23
“The goal of the Christian life is the acquisition of the Holy Spirit of God.”
— St. Seraphim of Sarov
“If the Holy Spirit were a creature, we would have no participation in God through Him.”
— St. Athanasius of Alexandria, Letters to Serapion
“No one has ever known God except insofar as the Holy Spirit illumined him.”
— St. Symeon the New Theologian
