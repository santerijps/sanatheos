Biblical Typology

A comprehensive guide to typological reading of Scripture—how Old Testament persons, events, and institutions prefigure their fulfillment in Christ, the Church, and the age to come.

What Is Typology?

Typology (from Greek typos, τύπος, meaning “stamp,” “pattern,” or “figure”) is the method of reading Scripture in which an Old Testament person, event, or institution (the type) is understood as a real, historical foreshadowing of a greater reality (the antitype) that is fulfilled in the New Testament—above all, in Christ.

Typology is not allegory. Allegory can treat the historical events as mere illustrations of timeless truths. Typology insists that the type is historically real—Adam really existed, the Exodus really happened, the Temple really stood—and that this real history was providentially ordered by God to point forward to its fulfillment. The antitype does not cancel the type; it completes it.

The New Testament authors themselves read the Old Testament typologically. Christ said, “As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up” (John 3:14). Paul wrote, “Adam… is a type (typos) of the one who was to come” (Romans 5:14). The entire liturgical and iconographic tradition of the Orthodox Church is saturated with typology.

Fathers

  • St. Irenaeus of Lyon — the first great systematizer of typology, showing how all of Old Testament history converges on Christ (Against Heresies).
  • St. Paul the Apostle — explicitly uses the term typos (Romans 5:14; 1 Corinthians 10:6, 11).
  • The Author of Hebrews — the entire letter is a typological argument: the Law, the priesthood, and the tabernacle are “shadows” of heavenly realities (Hebrews 8:5, 10:1).

Types of Christ (Persons)

Adam → Christ (the New Adam)

Adam is the first man, head of the old humanity. Christ is the New Adam, head of the new humanity. “For as in Adam all die, so in Christ all will be made alive” (1 Corinthians 15:22). Adam’s disobedience brought death; Christ’s obedience brings life. Adam fell in the Garden; Christ triumphed in the Garden of Gethsemane. Adam reached for the forbidden tree; Christ was lifted on the Tree of the Cross.

Genesis 2–3 → Romans 5:12–21; 1 Corinthians 15:21–22, 45–49

Eve → The Theotokos (the New Eve)

Eve, a virgin, listened to the word of a fallen angel and brought death. Mary, a virgin, listened to the word of the Archangel Gabriel and brought Life into the world. “The knot of Eve’s disobedience was loosened by the obedience of Mary” (St. Irenaeus). Eve is “the mother of all living” (Genesis 3:20) in the natural order; Mary is the mother of all living in the order of grace.

Genesis 3:1–6 → Luke 1:26–38; St. Irenaeus, Against Heresies III.22.4

Abel → Christ

Abel, the righteous shepherd, offered an acceptable sacrifice and was murdered by his brother out of envy. Christ, the Good Shepherd, offered the perfect sacrifice of Himself and was killed by His own people out of envy. Abel’s blood “cries out from the ground” (Genesis 4:10); Christ’s blood “speaks a better word than the blood of Abel” (Hebrews 12:24)—it cries not for vengeance but for mercy.

Genesis 4:1–10 → Hebrews 12:24; Matthew 23:35

Noah → Christ

Noah is the savior of his household through the waters of the flood, building an ark at God’s command. Christ saves the Church through the waters of baptism. “Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you” (1 Peter 3:21). The ark is a type of the Church: the one vessel of salvation floating on the waters of death. The dove returning with the olive branch prefigures the Holy Spirit descending at Christ’s baptism.

Genesis 6–9 → 1 Peter 3:18–22; Matthew 3:16

Melchizedek → Christ

Melchizedek, king of Salem, “priest of God Most High,” appeared mysteriously to Abraham and offered bread and wine (Genesis 14:18). He had no recorded genealogy, no beginning of days or end of life. The author of Hebrews sees in him a type of Christ: a priest not of the Levitical order but of an eternal, superior order. Christ is “a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek” (Psalm 110:4; Hebrews 5–7). The bread and wine prefigure the Eucharist.

Genesis 14:18–20; Psalm 110:4 → Hebrews 5:6–10, 6:20–7:28

Abraham’s Sacrifice of Isaac → The Crucifixion

Abraham was asked to sacrifice his only-begotten son (monogenes, the same word John uses for Christ in John 3:16) on Mount Moriah—traditionally identified with the site of the Temple and Golgotha. Isaac carried the wood for his own sacrifice up the mountain, as Christ carried His Cross. A ram caught in the thicket was substituted—God Himself provided the lamb. Abraham received Isaac back “as a figure” (en parabole, Hebrews 11:19) of resurrection.

Genesis 22:1–19 → John 3:16; Hebrews 11:17–19

Joseph → Christ

Joseph was beloved of his father, hated and sold by his brothers, descended into a pit (a kind of death), rose to become the savior of all Egypt (and of his own brothers who betrayed him), and forgave those who wronged him. He provided bread to the starving world. Nearly every detail of Joseph’s life is a type of Christ’s passion, descent to Hades, resurrection, exaltation, and universal salvation.

Genesis 37–50 → multiple Gospel parallels

Moses → Christ

Moses is the lawgiver, the deliverer, and the mediator between God and Israel. Christ is the new Moses who gives the new Law (the Sermon on the Mount, delivered on a mountain as at Sinai), delivers humanity from the bondage of sin and death, and mediates the new covenant in His blood. Moses fasted forty days on the mountain; Christ fasted forty days in the wilderness. Moses’ face shone with reflected glory (Exodus 34:29); Christ’s face shone with His own uncreated glory at the Transfiguration (Matthew 17:2).

Exodus – Deuteronomy → Matthew 5–7; Acts 3:22, 7:37; Hebrews 3:1–6

Joshua → Christ

Joshua (Yehoshua) literally bears the same name as Jesus (Iesous in Greek). He led Israel across the Jordan into the Promised Land; Christ leads humanity through the waters of baptism into the Kingdom of God. Joshua conquered Jericho by the power of God; Christ conquers death and Hades by the power of His resurrection.

Joshua 1–6 → Hebrews 4:8–10; Acts 7:45

David → Christ

David is the anointed king, the shepherd who defeats Goliath, the ancestor of the Messiah. Christ is the Son of David, the true King of Israel, the Shepherd who defeats the ultimate enemy (death), and the one who sits on David’s throne forever. David was born in Bethlehem; Christ was born in Bethlehem. David was a man after God’s own heart; Christ is the very Heart of God incarnate.

1–2 Samuel → Matthew 1:1, 12:23, 21:9; Luke 1:32–33; Acts 2:29–36

Solomon → Christ

Solomon built the Temple, the dwelling place of God among men. Christ said, “Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up” (John 2:19)—His body is the true Temple. Solomon was famed for his wisdom; Christ is the Wisdom of God (1 Corinthians 1:24). The Queen of Sheba came from the ends of the earth to hear Solomon; “something greater than Solomon is here” (Matthew 12:42).

1 Kings 3–10 → John 2:19–21; Matthew 12:42; 1 Corinthians 1:24

Elijah → John the Baptist / Christ

Elijah was the great prophet who called Israel to repentance, was fed miraculously, was taken up to heaven in a chariot of fire, and was expected to return before the Messiah (Malachi 4:5). Christ said that John the Baptist came “in the spirit and power of Elijah” (Luke 1:17; Matthew 11:14). Elijah appeared at the Transfiguration with Moses, representing the Prophets alongside the Law, both witnessing to Christ.

1 Kings 17–2 Kings 2; Malachi 4:5 → Matthew 11:14, 17:1–8; Luke 1:17

Jonah → Christ’s Death and Resurrection

Jonah spent three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish and then was vomited out alive. Christ Himself declared this a type of His own death and resurrection: “For just as Jonah was three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish, so will the Son of Man be three days and three nights in the heart of the earth” (Matthew 12:40). Jonah then preached repentance to the Gentiles (Nineveh); after His resurrection, Christ’s Gospel goes to all nations.

Jonah 1–3 → Matthew 12:39–41; Luke 11:29–32

The Suffering Servant → Christ

Isaiah’s Suffering Servant (Isaiah 52:13–53:12) is the supreme Old Testament prophecy-type: “He was wounded for our transgressions, He was bruised for our iniquities… and by His stripes we are healed.” The Ethiopian eunuch asked Philip, “About whom does the prophet say this?” and Philip “beginning from this Scripture, preached Jesus to him” (Acts 8:34–35).

Isaiah 52:13–53:12 → Acts 8:26–35; 1 Peter 2:21–25

Types of Christ (Events)

The Passover Lamb → Christ Crucified

In Egypt, the Israelites slaughtered a lamb without blemish, put its blood on the doorposts, and death “passed over” their homes (Exodus 12). Christ is “the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world” (John 1:29). He was crucified at Passover. Not one of the Passover lamb’s bones was to be broken (Exodus 12:46); not one of Christ’s bones was broken (John 19:36). Paul states it directly: “Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed” (1 Corinthians 5:7).

Exodus 12 → John 1:29, 19:36; 1 Corinthians 5:7; 1 Peter 1:18–19

The Exodus through the Red Sea → Baptism

Israel passed through the waters of the Red Sea, delivered from slavery in Egypt, and emerged on the other side as a free people. This is the supreme Old Testament type of Baptism: passage through water, death to the old life (Egypt = bondage to sin), and birth into new life. Paul says, “Our fathers were all baptized into Moses in the cloud and in the sea” (1 Corinthians 10:2).

Exodus 14 → 1 Corinthians 10:1–4; Romans 6:3–4

The Manna in the Wilderness → The Eucharist

God fed Israel with bread from heaven in the wilderness (Exodus 16). Christ declares: “I am the bread of life. Your fathers ate the manna in the wilderness, and they died. This is the bread that comes down from heaven, so that one may eat of it and not die” (John 6:48–50). The manna sustained physical life temporarily; the Eucharist gives eternal life.

Exodus 16 → John 6:30–58; 1 Corinthians 10:3

Water from the Rock → Christ

Moses struck the rock and water flowed to save the dying people (Exodus 17:6; Numbers 20:11). Paul says directly: “They drank from the spiritual Rock that followed them, and the Rock was Christ” (1 Corinthians 10:4). From Christ’s side, pierced on the Cross, flowed blood and water (John 19:34)—the Eucharist and Baptism.

Exodus 17:1–7; Numbers 20:2–13 → 1 Corinthians 10:4; John 19:34

The Brazen Serpent → The Cross

When the Israelites were bitten by serpents, God told Moses to make a bronze serpent and lift it on a pole; anyone who looked at it would live (Numbers 21:8–9). Christ Himself cited this as a type of His crucifixion: “As Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, that whoever believes in Him may have eternal life” (John 3:14–15). The instrument of death (the serpent) elevated becomes the instrument of healing—just as the Cross, an instrument of death, becomes the instrument of salvation.

Numbers 21:4–9 → John 3:14–15

The Crossing of the Jordan → Baptism / Entry into the Kingdom

Joshua led Israel across the Jordan River into the Promised Land. The waters parted as they had at the Red Sea. Christ was baptized in the Jordan, sanctifying its waters and opening the way into the Kingdom of God. The Jordan crossing completes the Exodus typology: Red Sea (baptism into death) → wilderness (the spiritual struggle) → Jordan (entrance into glory).

Joshua 3–4 → Matthew 3:13–17

The Fall of Jericho → The Destruction of Death

Jericho’s walls fell at the sound of seven trumpets and the shout of the people (Joshua 6). The Fathers see in this a type of the last day: at the sound of the trumpet (1 Thessalonians 4:16; 1 Corinthians 15:52), the walls of death and Hades collapse, and the people of God enter into their inheritance. Rahab the harlot, who hung a scarlet cord from her window (Joshua 2:18), is a type of the Church: she was a sinner who was saved by faith and by the sign of the red (the Blood of Christ).

Joshua 2, 6 → Hebrews 11:30–31; 1 Corinthians 15:52

The Flood → Baptism / Judgement

The great flood destroyed the old, corrupt world and through the ark, a remnant was saved through water into a new world. “Baptism, which corresponds to this, now saves you” (1 Peter 3:21). The eight souls saved in the ark prefigure the eighth day—the day of Resurrection, the day beyond the seven days of this world, the beginning of the new creation.

Genesis 6–9 → 1 Peter 3:18–22; 2 Peter 2:5

Types of the Theotokos

The Burning Bush → The Virgin Birth

The bush burned with fire but was not consumed (Exodus 3:2). The Fathers see in this a type of the Theotokos: she bore the divine fire—God Himself—in her womb and was not destroyed. Her virginity was not consumed by the Incarnation. This is one of the most beloved Marian types in Orthodox iconography and hymnography.

Exodus 3:1–6 → Orthodox hymns; St. Gregory of Nyssa, Life of Moses

The Ark of the Covenant → The Theotokos

The Ark of the Covenant contained the tablets of the Law (the Word of God written on stone), the manna (bread from heaven), and Aaron’s rod that budded (life out of dead wood) (Hebrews 9:4). Mary is the living Ark who carried the Word of God incarnate, the true Bread from Heaven, and the Life that sprang from her virgin womb. The Ark was overshadowed by the shekinah glory; Mary was “overshadowed” by the Holy Spirit (Luke 1:35).

Exodus 25:10–22; Hebrews 9:4 → Luke 1:35; Revelation 11:19–12:1

Jacob’s Ladder → The Theotokos

Jacob saw a ladder reaching from earth to heaven with angels ascending and descending (Genesis 28:12). The Fathers see Mary as the Ladder: through her, God descended to earth in the Incarnation, and through her, the way from earth to heaven was opened. Christ Himself alludes to this: “You will see heaven opened, and the angels of God ascending and descending on the Son of Man” (John 1:51).

Genesis 28:10–17 → John 1:51; Orthodox hymnography

The Closed Gate → The Ever-Virginity

Ezekiel saw a gate in the Temple that was shut: “This gate shall remain shut; it shall not be opened, and no one shall enter by it, for the Lord, the God of Israel, has entered by it; therefore it shall remain shut” (Ezekiel 44:2). The Fathers interpret this as a type of Mary’s perpetual virginity: the Lord entered the world through her, and she remained ever-virgin.

Ezekiel 44:1–3 → Orthodox Marian theology

Gideon’s Fleece → The Incarnation

Gideon laid out a fleece and the dew fell on it alone, while the ground around it remained dry; then the ground was wet and the fleece alone was dry (Judges 6:36–40). The Fathers see in this a type of the Incarnation: the dew of heaven (the Holy Spirit) descended silently and gently on Mary alone, filling her while the world around remained in darkness.

Judges 6:36–40 → Orthodox hymnography

The Mountain “Cut Without Hands” → The Virgin Birth

Daniel saw a stone cut from a mountain without human hands that crushed the statue of the kingdoms (Daniel 2:34–35, 44–45). The Fathers read the stone as Christ and the mountain as Mary: Christ came forth from her womb without the agency of a man (“cut without hands”) and will destroy all earthly kingdoms and establish the eternal Kingdom.

Daniel 2:31–45 → Orthodox Marian and Christological teaching

Types of the Church & the Sacraments

Noah’s Ark → The Church

The Ark is the one vessel of salvation in a dying world. Outside the Ark, all perish in the waters of judgment; inside, all are preserved alive. The Church is the Ark: the one Body of Christ in which humanity is saved from the flood of death and sin. “He cannot have God for his Father who has not the Church for his mother” (St. Cyprian).

Genesis 6–9 → 1 Peter 3:20–21; St. Cyprian, On the Unity of the Church

The Tabernacle / Temple → The Church / Christ’s Body

The Tabernacle and later the Temple were the dwelling place of God among His people. The shekinah glory filled them. Christ is the true Temple (“Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up,” John 2:19–21), and the Church is “the temple of the living God” (2 Corinthians 6:16). Each individual Christian is also a temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19).

Exodus 25–40; 1 Kings 6–8 → John 2:19–21; 1 Corinthians 3:16, 6:19; 2 Corinthians 6:16

The Levitical Priesthood → The Priesthood of Christ

The Levitical priests offered animal sacrifices daily, entering the holy places to mediate between God and the people. But these sacrifices could never take away sins (Hebrews 10:4). Christ is the true High Priest who offered Himself—once for all—and entered the true Holy of Holies (heaven itself) with His own blood (Hebrews 9:11–12). The entire Levitical system was “a shadow of the good things to come” (Hebrews 10:1).

Leviticus 1–16 → Hebrews 5–10

Circumcision → Baptism

Circumcision was the sign of the Old Covenant—the mark of belonging to God’s people, performed on the eighth day. Baptism is the sign of the New Covenant—the entry into the Body of Christ. Paul calls baptism “the circumcision made without hands” (Colossians 2:11–12). The eighth day symbolism persists: the day of resurrection is the “eighth day,” the new creation, and many baptismal fonts are octagonal.

Genesis 17:9–14 → Colossians 2:11–12; Romans 2:28–29

The Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) → The Cross

Once a year the High Priest entered the Holy of Holies with the blood of a goat to make atonement for the people (Leviticus 16). A second goat, the scapegoat, was sent into the wilderness bearing the sins of the people. Christ fulfills both: He is both the High Priest who enters the heavenly sanctuary and the Sacrifice whose blood cleanses, and He is the one who bears the sins of the world away (John 1:29).

Leviticus 16 → Hebrews 9:6–14, 24–28; John 1:29

The Sabbath → The Eternal Rest / The Lord’s Day

God rested on the seventh day (Genesis 2:2–3), and the Sabbath was given to Israel as a sign of the covenant. The Author of Hebrews reinterprets the Sabbath rest as a type of the eschatological rest: “There remains a Sabbath rest for the people of God” (Hebrews 4:9). Christians worship on Sunday, the eighth day / first day of the new creation—the day of the Resurrection. Christ Himself is our Sabbath rest.

Genesis 2:2–3; Exodus 20:8–11 → Hebrews 4:1–11; Colossians 2:16–17

Types of the Cross & Salvation

The Tree of Life → The Cross

In Eden, the Tree of Life stood at the center of the garden (Genesis 2:9), and access to it was barred after the Fall (Genesis 3:24). The Cross is the new Tree of Life: by it, death is destroyed and access to eternal life is restored. “Blessed are those who… have the right to the tree of life” (Revelation 22:14). By a tree came death; by the Tree of the Cross comes life—a parallel the liturgy celebrates constantly.

Genesis 2:9, 3:22–24 → Revelation 2:7, 22:2, 14; liturgical hymns

Moses’ Outstretched Arms → The Cross

When Israel fought Amalek, Moses stood on a hill with his arms raised. As long as his arms were up, Israel prevailed; when they dropped, Amalek prevailed (Exodus 17:11–12). The Fathers see in Moses’ outstretched arms the shape of the Cross: Christ, with arms stretched out on the Cross, conquers the enemy of the human race.

Exodus 17:8–16 → patristic exegesis; St. Justin Martyr, Dialogue with Trypho 90

The Scarlet Cord of Rahab → The Blood of Christ

Rahab the harlot hung a scarlet cord from her window, and her household was saved when Jericho fell (Joshua 2:18, 6:25). The Fathers see in the scarlet cord a type of the Blood of Christ: the sign of red, displayed in faith, is the mark of salvation. Rahab, a Gentile sinner, is saved by faith and becomes an ancestor of Christ (Matthew 1:5).

Joshua 2:18–21, 6:22–25 → Hebrews 11:31; Matthew 1:5; St. Clement of Rome, 1 Clement 12

Elisha’s Wood in the Water → The Cross

When an axe head fell into the Jordan River, the prophet Elisha threw a stick of wood into the water, and the iron floated (2 Kings 6:5–7). The Fathers see in this a type of the Cross: when the wood of the Cross entered the waters of death, what had sunk (fallen human nature) was raised up.

2 Kings 6:1–7 → patristic exegesis

The Wood Cast into Marah → The Cross

When the waters of Marah were bitter and the people could not drink, God showed Moses a tree (xylon), and when he cast it into the water, the water became sweet (Exodus 15:23–25). The Fathers see in this the Cross transforming the bitterness of death into the sweetness of life.

Exodus 15:22–25 → patristic exegesis; St. Justin Martyr, Dialogue 86

Additional Types

The Creation of Eve from Adam’s Side → The Church from Christ’s Side

God caused Adam to fall into a deep sleep and from his side took a rib and formed Eve (Genesis 2:21–22). Christ fell into the “sleep” of death on the Cross, and from His side flowed blood and water (John 19:34)—the Eucharist and Baptism, from which the Church, His Bride, is born. As Eve was bone of Adam’s bone and flesh of his flesh, so the Church is the Body of Christ.

Genesis 2:21–23 → John 19:34; Ephesians 5:25–32

The Coat of Skins → Christ’s Assumed Humanity

After the Fall, God clothed Adam and Eve in garments of skin (Genesis 3:21). The Fathers read this typologically in various ways: the skins represent mortality (the condition human nature took on after the Fall), which Christ assumed in the Incarnation in order to heal and transfigure it. Christ put on our “garments of skin”—our mortal flesh—so that we might put on the garment of incorruption.

Genesis 3:21 → St. Gregory of Nyssa, On the Making of Man; St. Maximus the Confessor

The Pillar of Cloud and Fire → The Holy Spirit / Christ

God guided Israel through the wilderness with a pillar of cloud by day and a pillar of fire by night (Exodus 13:21–22). The cloud both reveals and conceals God’s presence—a figure of the divine mystery. The cloud overshadowed the Tabernacle (Exodus 40:34) as the Holy Spirit overshadowed Mary (Luke 1:35). At the Transfiguration, a cloud overshadowed the disciples (Matthew 17:5).

Exodus 13:21–22, 40:34–38 → Luke 1:35; Matthew 17:5; 1 Corinthians 10:1–2

The Twelve Tribes → The Twelve Apostles

Israel was constituted as God’s people through twelve tribes descended from Jacob’s twelve sons. Christ chose twelve apostles to constitute the new Israel, the Church. “You who have followed Me will also sit on twelve thrones, judging the twelve tribes of Israel” (Matthew 19:28). The twelve foundations of the heavenly Jerusalem bear the names of the twelve apostles (Revelation 21:14).

Genesis 49; Exodus 24:4 → Matthew 10:1–4, 19:28; Revelation 21:12–14

The Covenant with Abraham → The New Covenant

God made a covenant with Abraham, promising him descendants as numerous as the stars and a land flowing with milk and honey (Genesis 15, 17). This covenant was sealed with circumcision and sacrifice. The New Covenant is sealed with baptism and the sacrifice of Christ. The true descendants of Abraham are those who share his faith: “If you are Christ’s, then you are Abraham’s offspring, heirs according to promise” (Galatians 3:29).

Genesis 12, 15, 17 → Galatians 3:6–29; Romans 4; Hebrews 6:13–20

The Three Visitors to Abraham → The Holy Trinity

At the Oak of Mamre, three men appeared to Abraham, and he bowed to them as to the Lord (Genesis 18:1–3). The Fathers (and the famous icon by St. Andrei Rublev) see in this a type of the Holy Trinity: three Persons, one God. Abraham offered them a meal of bread, a calf, curds, and milk—a type of the Eucharistic hospitality where God comes to dwell with His people.

Genesis 18:1–15 → Rublev’s Trinity Icon; patristic tradition

The Suffering of Job → The Suffering of Christ / The Cross

Job, a righteous man, suffered unjustly, was stripped of everything, sat on an ash heap covered in wounds, was mocked by his friends, and yet maintained his faith. He is a type of Christ, the perfectly righteous One who suffered unjustly, was stripped, wounded, mocked, and yet entrusted Himself to the Father. Job was ultimately vindicated and restored with far more than he had before—a type of the Resurrection and exaltation of Christ.

Job 1–42 → patristic exegesis; read on Holy Week in the Orthodox Church

The Three Young Men in the Furnace → The Resurrection

Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego were thrown into the fiery furnace by Nebuchadnezzar but were preserved unharmed, and a fourth figure appeared with them “like a son of the gods” (Daniel 3:25). The Fathers see in this a type of Christ’s descent into death/Hades (the furnace) and resurrection: the fire of death cannot consume those who are united to the Son of God. The three youths are also a type of the Christian martyrs preserved by grace in the midst of persecution.

Daniel 3 → patristic exegesis; Orthodox liturgical canons

Daniel in the Lions’ Den → Christ in Hades

Daniel was thrown into the den of lions, sealed with a stone, and emerged alive on the next day (Daniel 6). Christ descended into Hades, the realm of death (sealed with a stone before the tomb), and rose on the third day. The lions could not devour Daniel because God “shut the lions’ mouths”; death could not hold Christ because “it was not possible for Him to be held by it” (Acts 2:24).

Daniel 6 → Acts 2:24; patristic exegesis

Ruth the Moabite → The Gentile Church

Ruth, a Gentile woman, left her own people and gods to cling to Naomi and to the God of Israel: “Your people shall be my people, and your God my God” (Ruth 1:16). She married Boaz, the kinsman-redeemer (goel), and became an ancestor of David and Christ (Matthew 1:5). Ruth is a type of the Gentile Church grafted into Israel by faith, and Boaz is a type of Christ, the true Kinsman-Redeemer who takes the outsider as His bride.

Ruth 1–4 → Matthew 1:5; Romans 11:17–24

Esther → The Theotokos / The Church

Esther, a humble maiden, became queen and interceded before the king for her condemned people, risking her own life to save them from destruction (Esther 4–7). She is a type of the Theotokos, who intercedes before the King of Heaven for the human race, and also of the Church, the Bride of Christ who intercedes for the world.

Esther 4–8 → Orthodox Marian and ecclesiological typology

The Exodus from Egypt → Salvation from Sin and Death

The entire Exodus narrative is the master typological sequence: Egypt = bondage to sin; Pharaoh = the devil; Moses = Christ the Deliverer; the Red Sea = Baptism; the Wilderness = the ascetic struggle; Manna = the Eucharist; the Pillar of Cloud/Fire = the Holy Spirit; the Law at Sinai = the New Commandment; the Tabernacle = the Church; the Promised Land = the Kingdom of God. This is why the Exodus readings dominate the Orthodox Holy Saturday liturgy.

Exodus 1–40 → 1 Corinthians 10:1–11; Hebrews 3–4; Holy Saturday Vesperal Liturgy
